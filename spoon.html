Self-Stabilizing Assistive Spoon

Active Tremor Compensation using PID Control and Sensory Fusion

Project Overview

Developed as a final project for the Control & Automation Lab I at UFMG, this device is an assistive technology solution designed to help individuals with involuntary tremors (such as Parkinson's or Essential Tremor) regain autonomy while eating.

The system functions as a handheld 2-axis gimbal, monitoring the spoon's orientation in real-time and adjusting internal servomotors to counteract manual instability. By maintaining the utensil's level relative to the initial reference, the device prevents food from spilling during use.

Demo: Watch on YouTube

Technical Architecture

  • Hardware & Sensing: Powered by an ESP32 microcontroller and an MPU-6050 inertial measurement unit (IMU). Actuation is handled by two 9g servomotors managing Roll (X-axis) and Pitch (Y-axis).
  • Signal Processing: Implementation of a Kalman Filter to fuse accelerometer and gyroscope data, providing a stable, low-noise angular estimation for the feedback loop.
  • System Identification: Conducted experimental step-response tests analyzed in MATLAB. We utilized the ident toolbox to fit Output Error (OE) polynomial models, enabling a mathematical understanding of the plant's dynamics and transport delays.
  • Control Strategy: A PID Controller was designed via Loop Shaping and tuned experimentally. The final implementation prioritizes smooth movement and stability to ensure user comfort.
  • Supervisory Interface: Developed a real-time SCADA interface using Elipse E3. Communication is established via Modbus TCP over Wi-Fi, allowing for live monitoring of angles and on-the-fly tuning of PID gains without reflashing the firmware.

Results & Development

The project successfully integrated mechanical design (3D printing), electronic hardware, and control theory. By utilizing an Extended Kalman Filter and a closed-loop PID architecture, the prototype achieved high reliability in rejecting external disturbances. The addition of the Modbus supervisory layer was a key differentiator, significantly accelerating the calibration phase by allowing us to observe the immediate impact of parameter changes on the physical hardware.
