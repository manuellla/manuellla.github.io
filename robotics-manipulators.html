Industrial Manipulator Modeling & Control

Precision Kinematics and Trajectory Tracking with the Comau SmartSix

Overview

This project involved the complete kinematic modeling and control implementation for a Comau SmartSix industrial manipulator. The primary objective was to execute high-precision Cartesian paths, including a stylized Brazilian flag in a simulated environment and a geometric rectangle on a physical robot.

Kinematic Modeling

The foundation of the project was the derivation of the robot’s geometry using the Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) convention. By defining local coordinate systems for each of the six revolute joints, we established the individual transformation matrices $A_i$ to compute the forward kinematics:

$${}^{0}T_{6} = A_{1}A_{2}A_{3}A_{4}A_{5}A_{6}$$

A critical step involved aligning the physical robot's base—which is oriented downward ($Z_0$) in the laboratory—with the virtual simulation environment by applying a $180^\circ$ rotation ($R_{x}(\pi)$) to the base frame to ensure physical-to-virtual consistency.

Control Strategies & Implementation

We implemented two distinct control architectures in MATLAB using the Robotics Toolbox to manage the end-effector’s pose:

  • Regulation Control: Utilized for point-to-point positioning, such as moving from the home state to the initial drawing coordinates. This proportional controller minimizes the pose error $\tilde{X}$ through the inverse Jacobian $J^{-1}$:

    $$u(t) = J^{-1}(K\tilde{X})$$

  • Trajectory Tracking: To render the complex geometry of the flag, we employed a feedforward velocity approach. This ensured the robot adhered to strict temporal constraints while following parametric equations for the rectangle, rhombus, and circle:

    $$u(t) = J^{-1}(\dot{X}_{d} + K\tilde{X})$$

Simulation & Physical Deployment

The system was validated through co-simulation between MATLAB and CoppeliaSim, where we monitored joint positions, control actions, and Euclidean error to confirm asymptotic stability.

For real-world deployment, we programmed the robot using PDL2 on a Comau C4G controller. This stage included a comparative analysis of MOVE JOINT versus MOVE LINEAR commands, demonstrating that while joint-space motion offers speed for repositioning, Cartesian-linear interpolation is essential for maintaining the geometric integrity of precision paths.

Environment: MATLAB, CoppeliaSim, WinC4G
